Background

CSFB Pest Action

How the Cabbage Stem Flea Beetle's annual cycle is synchronised with oilseed rape — and why it attacks at the worst possible time.

Overview

The Interaction Between CSFB and WOSR

The lifecycle of Psylliodes chrysocephala (CSFB) and Brassica napus (WOSR) are intimately synchronised. CSFB times its adult emergence and migration to coincide precisely with the period when WOSR is most biologically vulnerable — the seedling establishment phase.

Critical overlap: Peak CSFB adult migration (September–November) coincides exactly with OSR seedling establishment — the crop's most vulnerable window, before it reaches the protective 4–5 leaf stage.

March – May

Pupation

Larvae exit the host plant and pupate in the soil for 8–12 weeks, developing into adult beetles ready to emerge in early summer.

December – February

Egg Hatching & Host Entry

Eggs hatch after approximately 37 days and larvae penetrate the host plant to begin internal feeding. Hatching time is temperature-dependent:

at 10°C

June – August

Feeding & Summer Diapause

Adults emerge and feed briefly but intensively on mature OSR crops, accumulating energy reserves. They then migrate to sheltered, moist areas and enter aestivation — a 25-day period of summer diapause.

WhyTo reduce desiccation risk during summer
WhenIn anticipation of unfavourable conditions (high temperatures)
HowCSFB are univoltine insects — aestivation is obligate (essential) and genetically predetermined
WhatReduction in metabolic rate (↓ VCO₂ and NADH dehydrogenase abundance) and behavioural activity
September – November

Migration & Egg-Laying

Adults emerge from aestivation and migrate up to 2–3 miles (3–4 km) to find young WOSR seedlings. Peak migration occurs in mid-September when air temperature exceeds 16°C.

How CSFB locate WOSR:

  • Olfactory: WOSR releases ITCs (isothiocyanates) and GLSs (glucosinolates) in response to cell damage. Females show enhanced sensitivity to ITCs.
  • Visual cues and chance encounters

Colonising population is typically ~60% male. Adult flight muscles are lost through atrophy. Females lay eggs near the base of WOSR plants in the soil over a 7-month period.

Host Crop

OSR Annual Life Cycle

Click a season label on the diagram to explore that stage

March – May

Flowering & Pod Formation

The crop enters rapid growth phase. Yellow flowers bloom across fields — the iconic spring sight — followed by pod formation as pollination occurs. This is the most visually striking period of OSR's cycle.

December – February

Stem Elongation

Plants continue to grow through winter as stems elongate and the crop develops. However, CSFB larvae that hatched in autumn continue their internal feeding throughout this period, mining stems and weakening the plant's structure.

June – August

Harvest

The mature crop is harvested. Pods are collected and processed to extract rapeseed oil — the UK's most widely used cooking oil — and produce protein-rich meal for livestock feed.

September – November ⚠

Seedling Establishment

Most vulnerable period. This window coincides exactly with peak CSFB adult migration, exposing young seedlings to maximum pest pressure before they reach the protective 4–5 leaf stage.

Freshly sown seed germinates and seedlings emerge from the soil. Adult CSFB feeding on cotyledons and young leaves at this stage can stunt growth severely or kill plants outright.

Mechanism of Damage

How CSFB Damage WOSR

CSFB pose a two-level problem against WOSR, with adults and larvae inducing distinct and compounding effects at different times of year.

Adult Damage

Surface feeding on young leaves

When Late August to mid-September — peak damage coincides with peak adult migration
How Adults feed on young leaves and cotyledons, creating characteristic "shot-holing" patterns
Impact Stunts WOSR growth and slows crop establishment. Most severe before the 4–5 leaf stage, during seedling emergence. Can destroy growing plants entirely.

Larval Damage

Internal feeding inside stems

When Late autumn through to spring, continuing all winter
How Larvae hatch and burrow into WOSR plants, mining the petioles and side stems until they reach the main stem — internal feeding entirely hidden from view
Impact Weakens and thins the main stem, which can collapse plants and stunt growth. Internal damage also increases susceptibility to fungal pathogens: light leaf spot, phoma, and botrytis.
The Challenge

Why Are CSFB Difficult to Control

Several biological traits make CSFB uniquely challenging to manage — especially without a systemic seed treatment, where larvae burrow out of reach of all contact-acting foliar insecticides.

Click a point below to see why it matters

  • 10months

    Adults can live up to 10 months, providing a prolonged window to cause damage and lay eggs.

  • 200+eggs

    Each female can lay 200+ eggs, rapidly building population numbers even from a small initial infestation.

  • Larvae burrow inside stems, completely out of reach of contact-acting foliar insecticides.

  • CSFB are only accessible to pesticides during the brief adult feeding phase before they enter the plant.

  • CSFB can survive on related Brassica weeds between crops, maintaining populations even without OSR present.

  • Adapts quickly to pesticide exposure through both knockdown resistance mutations and metabolic resistance mechanisms.

  • Without a systemic seed treatment (banned since 2013), larvae inside stems are completely unreachable by any available pesticide.

  • Adults move quickly from plant to plant and can readily escape natural enemies, making biological control difficult.
